Here is a detailed description of the Prodigy framework:
Prodigy is a comprehensive framework synthesising the main concepts, ideas, and best practices of what is considered "productive dialogue". It consists of 15 features clustered to the four dimensions of productive dialogue: Content, Interpersonal Rapport, Perspective-taking and listening, and Trustworthiness.
A) Content. This dimension relates to different aspects of how (scientific) knowledge is communicated and presented. This includes the extent to which topical content is presented in a clear and informative way so that it can be understood by the intended audience easily and correctly, to which it is backed by rational reasoning and valid arguments, and to which it uses reliable sources of information.
The features included in this dimension are:
•	a-1: Reasoning: Using evidence, arguments, and logic to establish conclusions or make judgments.
For example, in a discussion about composite materials, the speaker was asked to explain why in some cases it is preferable to use composite materials and not strong metals. The speaker then answered: “Metals are strong, but they are also heavy. And sometimes, they’re not as flexible as needed. Composite materials offer us a solution that balances strength and weight.”. This answer demonstrates the ”Reasoning” feature, since it addresses the logic underlying the use of composite materials.
•	a-2: Explaining: Clarifying by reformulating sentences or phrases, Socratic questions, contrasting, giving examples, anecdotes, analogies and metaphors.
For example, here is a quote demonstrating the “Explaining” feature: “I can think of a composite material that we’ve all known for thousands of years but don’t often think about—houses in Africa made of straw and mud! The straw is strong along its fiber direction, and the mud that surrounds it dries into a solid form, binding it all together. This creates a strong house from simple materials found even in local villages without advanced technology in Africa.” This quote includes the use of a simple example that a layperson without previous knowledge can understand, that explains the meaning of a complex concept (composite material).
•	a-3: Clarity: Increasing the partner's potential to understand the message by using lexical clarifications, avoiding jargon, and being concise.
For example, here is a quote in which the speaker defines the meaning of complex concepts (such as “fiber” and “matrix”), and does not assume that his partner understands them: “One of them, called the "fiber," gives the material its strength—you can think of it as a very strong thread. The other, called the "matrix," holds the fibers together and protects them. When combined, you get a material that is both strong and lightweight.”.
•	a-4: Tailoring: Adaptation, tailoring the message to the characteristics of the target audience (age, culture, prior knowledge, etc), and appropriate framing.
For example, in a discussion about quantum computing, the speaker was asked to explain its applications to a group of high school students. They said: "Imagine you have a massive library, and you’re looking for one specific book. Normally, you’d have to check each shelf one by one. But with quantum computing, it’s like having a magical system that can check all the shelves at once, finding your book much faster. It’s especially useful for solving puzzles like climate modelling or medicine discovery that need this kind of speed." This response demonstrates "Tailoring" by adapting the explanation to the students' age and knowledge level, using an analogy they could easily relate to.
•	a-5: Credibility: Using and referring to reliable knowledge sources.
For example: “In fact, an article was published a few months ago about a research conducted at Harvard showing that radiologists who use artificial intelligence to interpret patient scans don’t achieve better results than radiologists who don’t use AI.” This example demonstrates how referencing a specific study from a reputable academic institution enhances the speaker's credibility and makes their argument more compelling.
B) Interpersonal rapport. One can be very clear and articulate, but also unpleasant or antagonistic. Therefore, the second dimension of productive dialogue refers to how linguistic and other communication cues build or harm interpersonal rapport. The role of interpersonal rapport dialogue is twofold: as an outcome measure, i.e., rapport and interpersonal relations improve, or deteriorate, through dialogue, and as a moderator for the effects of dialogue, i.e., when participants feel respected and included, they are assumed to be more receptive to listen, learn, and reconsider their own views and ideas. The features of dialogue in this dimension are subtle, such as demonstrating benevolence, respecting different viewpoints, and being empathic and polite (e.g., saving face threats when disagreeing).
The features included in this dimension are:
•	b-1: Stressing similarities: Invoking and making references to shared values or common background, using inclusive language ("we" instead of "you"), giving examples from the partner's world.
For example, here is a quote demonstrating the “Stressing similarities” feature: “Just like you shared about the challenges you face in competitive wall climbing, I can totally relate – I deal with my own set of challenges in research. And, just like in climbing, when you finally succeed and reach the top, that sense of accomplishment is absolutely amazing”.
•	b-2: Empathy and Benevolence: Being empathic and polite, avoiding accusatory, competitive or prescriptive language, conveying kindness, goodwill, and concern.
For example, during a conversation about the ethical implications of genetic engineering, a participant expressed concern about designer babies. The speaker responded:
"I completely understand why this topic might worry you. It’s natural to feel uneasy when we’re talking about such profound changes to human life. Please know that scientists are also thinking about these concerns, and many of us are working hard to ensure that ethical guidelines are strictly followed." This demonstrates "Empathy and Benevolence" by acknowledging the listener’s concerns with kindness and without dismissing their fears.
•	b-3: Respect: Acknowledging the validity of different viewpoints each participant brings to the table.
For example, in a debate about renewable energy, the interlocutor argued that wind farms might harm local wildlife. The speaker replied: "That’s a valid point, and it’s something that scientists and environmentalists are actively working to address. Your perspective highlights an important aspect of the conversation—finding a balance between advancing technology and preserving nature." This response demonstrates "Respect" by acknowledging the other’s viewpoint as important and worth addressing.
•	b-4: Sharing personal details: Sharing personal experiences, anecdotes, or narratives to create a personal connection with the interlocutor.
For example: “I've always loved engineering things and finding creative solutions to problems. This passion drives me to engage in research, and I'm glad to see that you're interested as well.” Another Example: “Personally, I really, really love neurons and all the research surrounding them. I also know that one of our weaknesses in science today is that we don’t fully understand the nervous system. So, I wanted to enter a field where there’s still room to grow. My advisor specifically directed me toward the worm model, explained its advantages, and, of course, provided me with the tools to understand and work with this model.”.
C) Perspective-taking and listening. One can be respectful, and yet not invest in or engage with the ideas, thinking and perspectives of one's conversational partner. In productive dialogue, interlocutors actively listen to one another, engage with each other's thinking, and ideas, and try to understand and see the world through their partners’ eyes (perspective-taking). Since the attempts to mentalise the other participants’ thinking processes are covert, in this dimension, we look for external evidence for such inner processes – paraphrasing each other’s words, inviting them to share thoughts, and building on the partner’s ideas.
The features included in this dimension are:
•	c-1: Paraphrasing: Paraphrasing (revoicing) someone else's contribution in own words, to confirm understanding, clarify meaning, or highlight it.
For example, in a discussion about AI in healthcare, the listener said, "I’m worried that AI might replace doctors and lead to more mistakes." The speaker responded: "So, if I understand correctly, you’re concerned that relying on AI could reduce the human touch in medicine and possibly increase the risk of errors. Is that right?" This illustrates "Paraphrasing," as the speaker revoiced the listener’s concern to confirm understanding and encourage further clarification.
•	c-2: Invitations to share: Actively inviting a partner to contribute their opinions, ideas and thinking.
“Now I’d like to ask you if there’s anything I didn’t explain, or something you’re curious to know. Maybe I should have explained certain concepts more clearly, or perhaps you understood everything. Or is there something else you wish to share with me?”
“So I'm curious to think about how we can address the two challenges we presented: one being the hesitation, stemming from genuine fear about this topic, and on the other hand, how we can train teachers. How did you feel about this when you were a student?”
•	c-3: Building on the partner’s ideas: Acknowledging the partner's contributions, and transacting on them by continuing their ideas.
For instance, during a discussion on integrating AI into healthcare decision-making, ChatGPT suggested that doctors should be involved in the system's development. In response, the speaker replied: “Yes, for example, we can involve doctors in determining the threshold at which artificial intelligence should have confidence in itself, ensuring that it makes no more errors than the maximum a doctor can afford to tolerate.” This demonstrates how the speaker actively engaged with the partner’s idea, expanded upon it, and added meaningful elaboration.
Another example: “I think your ideas are truly excellent. Not all of them can actually be implemented—for example, ensuring some sort of collaboration where teachers receive compensation from the Ministry of Education is a more challenging goal. But perhaps starting with smaller steps could be effective. Even if it’s not a full lesson plan, something partial that allows the teacher to start introducing... And various ideas, such as starting a lesson in a research-based or critical-thinking approach, on topics related to science and chemistry, could be really, really valuable.”
D) Trustworthiness. Incorporation of the latter three dimensions into the dialogue should result in trust-building, but another important aspect of dialogue is whether the participants in the dialogue are worthy of trust. The fourth dimension emphasises ethical and responsible dialogue participation. SC literature notes that goals such as perception change and rebuilding a collective understanding of knowledge (e.g., Priest et al., 2019; Weingart & Guenther, 2016). can only be achieved if participants are transparent about their position, expertise, and values, admit and tolerate uncertainty, and show intellectual humility.
The features included in this dimension are:
•	d-1: Intellectual humility: Acknowledging the limitations of one’s knowledge and expertise, recognizing that one does not know everything or has all the answers.
“But it's important for me to note that in biology, there are many limitations, and part of our strength as researchers is approaching our work with an awareness of these limitations. In other words, a researcher who thinks they know everything is, in my view, making a very big mistake from the start. We understand that there is a limitation here, and that the leap from worms to humans is enormous.”
•	d-2: Transparency: Disclosing one’s position and values towards the subject/topic of conversation.
For example, during a discussion about the use of nuclear energy, the speaker said: “I should share that I’m personally in favor of nuclear energy as a solution to our energy crisis. This perspective is shaped by my background in environmental engineering, where I’ve seen firsthand how effective nuclear power can be in reducing carbon emissions. That said, I recognize there are valid concerns about waste management and safety, and I’m open to discussing those.” This example demonstrates “Transparency” by disclosing the speaker’s personal stance and explaining how their background has influenced their viewpoint, while remaining open to alternative perspectives.
•	d-3: Tolerating uncertainty: Accepting that not all information is available or conclusive, acknowledging that some questions may not have clear or immediate answers.
“That's an excellent question, and it's a question that shows me you're not from the field — but it's a good question. Why do I say that? Because you can't really take a human brain and experiment with it in the same way we experiment with worms. First of all, there are ethical regulations that apply to humans, which, as of now, do not apply to worms. This allows me to perform genetic manipulations and various specific analyses on nerve cells in worms that cannot be done in humans. Of course, we must also remember that the human system is far, far more complex. For example, if I’m talking about a neuron called AVA in a worm, and this neuron shows a pattern of reduced activity as a result of aging, there’s no exact equivalent of this in humans. In other words, a person reading the results of the paper wouldn’t be able to point to the human brain and identify what corresponds to AVA, because that neuron simply doesn’t exist in humans.”
Another example: “Unfortunately, in the scientific community today, we don’t really know how to provide explanations that are good enough to be considered true or even simplified yet accurate. We can say various things that might sound convincing to doctors, but that don’t actually have much to do with how artificial intelligence makes decisions. For this reason, I don’t feel comfortable offering such an explanation to doctors. I know in advance that it doesn’t hold much value for the doctor’s decision-making and is mostly meant to reassure the expert. I’d rather have the doctor live with this tension and understand that this gap between the reliability of AI and its results exists. I would explain to them how I address this tension mathematically and encourage them to remain open-minded to better solutions that might emerge in the future, because right now, I don’t have anything better to offer.”
